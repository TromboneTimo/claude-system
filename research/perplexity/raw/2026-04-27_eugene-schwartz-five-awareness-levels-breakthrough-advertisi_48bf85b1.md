# Eugene Schwartz five awareness levels (Breakthrough Advertising 1966): exact 5 levels, modern marketing application. Origin of name the unnamed pain copywriting principle.

## Key findings

**Eugene Schwartz outlined the five levels of customer awareness in his 1966 book *Breakthrough Advertising*.** The exact levels, from least to most aware, are: **Completely Unaware**, **Problem Aware**, **Solution Aware**, **Product Aware**, and **Most Aware**.[1][2][3][4][5]

### Original Source
Schwartz introduced these levels in *Breakthrough Advertising* (1966), where he systematized customer awareness to guide copywriting by matching messaging to what prospects already know.[2][3][4] The book emphasizes starting with the prospect's existing knowledge rather than assuming ignorance.[2]

### Modern Marketing Applications
These levels are widely used in content marketing, DTC advertising, and sales funnels to tailor messaging:
- **Unaware**: Use storytelling to reveal hidden problems, building reach and engagement.[1][3][5][6]
- **Problem Aware**: Highlight the pain point to make it urgent, e.g., "Do you know obesity is the number 1 cause of heart attack?"[3]
- **Solution Aware**: Introduce general solutions without pushing a specific product.[1][3]
- **Product Aware**: Compare your product to alternatives and address objections.[3][6]
- **Most Aware**: Focus on offers, urgency, and barriers to close sales.[1][2][6]

Marketers apply this as a funnel continuum, moving prospects from top (Unaware) to bottom (Most Aware).[1][2][5]

### Origin of "Name the Unnamed Pain" or "Name the Invisible Problem"
This copywriting principle—articulating a prospect's unexpressed or unrecognized problem—is most directly tied to **Eugene Schwartz**. He described naming subtle desires or "unnamed" market needs in *Breakthrough Advertising*, influencing headlines that reveal hidden pains to elevate awareness.[2][3] No search results attribute it explicitly to Joe Sugarman, Gary Halbert, or Dan Kennedy; it aligns with Schwartz's core framework for Unaware and Problem Aware stages.[1][3] Later copywriters like Halbert built on it, but Schwartz originated the foundational idea.[2]
