# Conversion Triggers Taxonomy

A conversion trigger is the specific psychological switch that flips someone from "interested" to "buying". Read this BEFORE synthesizing the 5 ideas, so each idea is mapped to a named trigger.

This taxonomy is built from the Precision Brass corpus. It is not exhaustive. Add new triggers when you find them in the research subagent outputs.

## The named triggers

### T1. "Someone finally named what's wrong with me"
The prospect has lived with a vague, embarrassing problem for years. Possibly decades. They couldn't say it out loud because they didn't have words for it. Harrison gives them words. The relief is immediate and emotional.

**Activates when:** content describes the prospect's exact symptom in language they recognize but couldn't generate themselves.

**Example phrases from corpus:** "I've never heard anyone explain it that way", "that's exactly what's been happening to me", "I thought I was the only one"

**Risk:** if the naming is too generic ("you're frustrated"), it doesn't trigger. Must be specific ("your top lip is doing 80% of the work and your bottom lip is along for the ride").

### T2. "Failed methods get named and dismissed"
Comeback players have a graveyard of methods they've tried. Caruso. Stamp. Clarke. Carmine Caruso again. James Stamp again. Every YouTube tutorial. They feel stupid that they can't make any of it work. The trigger fires when Harrison validates that the method itself is the problem, not the prospect.

**Activates when:** content explicitly addresses why a respected, mainstream method fails for the comeback player and what's missing.

**Example phrases from corpus:** "I've tried everything", "I did Stamp for two years and got nowhere", "I'm not stupid I just can't get this to work"

### T3. "A specific demonstration that contradicts what they were taught"
Visual proof. Harrison plays something effortlessly that violates the rules they were taught. The prospect's internal model breaks. Curiosity floods in.

**Activates when:** content includes an on-camera demonstration that visibly contradicts a widely-held trumpet belief.

**Example phrases from corpus:** "I couldn't believe how easy that looked", "you're not even trying and you're hitting double C", "wait, that's not how I was taught"

### T4. "Identity restoration, not skill acquisition"
The prospect doesn't want to learn trumpet. They want to be the player they were at 22. Or the player they should have been if their first teacher had known better. Or the player their college bandmates respected. The trigger is reclamation, not novelty.

**Activates when:** content frames the outcome as "get back" or "recover" or "finally play like" rather than "learn" or "improve".

**Example phrases from corpus:** "I just want to play like I used to", "I want to be that guy again", "I had a high G at 22 and now I can't even hit a C"

### T5. "Permission to stop chasing equipment"
Many prospects have wasted thousands of dollars on mouthpieces. They've cycled through 30 of them. They're embarrassed. The trigger fires when Harrison gives them permission to stop and tells them the mouthpiece isn't the problem.

**Activates when:** content directly addresses mouthpiece chasing as a symptom, not a solution, and offers a technique-first reframe.

**Example phrases from corpus:** "I have 47 mouthpieces", "I keep buying gear hoping it'll fix it", "my wife would kill me if she knew how much I spent"

### T6. "The age frame is broken"
The prospect believes their window has closed. Dental work, lip damage, advancing age, scar tissue. The trigger fires when content shows a peer (60+, comeback player, post-dental) succeeding in a specific named way.

**Activates when:** content features a relatable older success story OR explicitly disputes the "too late" frame with mechanical reasoning.

**Example phrases from corpus:** "I thought I was too old", "after my dental work I figured it was over", "if I can do it at 67, you can do it"

### T7. "Isolation breaks"
Many prospects practice alone. No teacher in 30 years. No section. The trigger fires when content makes them feel SEEN by someone who understands their specific isolation.

**Activates when:** content acknowledges the loneliness of the comeback player and explicitly addresses the lack of feedback as the silent killer.

**Example phrases from corpus:** "nobody to ask", "I haven't had a teacher in decades", "I practice in the basement and my wife yells at me"

### T8. "Permission to want it"
A subtle one. Many older players feel guilty wanting to be excellent at trumpet. They feel like it's frivolous, indulgent, or that they should be doing something more "responsible". The trigger fires when Harrison reframes the want as legitimate, masculine, identity-essential.

**Activates when:** content names the guilt and dismisses it explicitly. Often paired with T4.

**Example phrases from corpus:** "my wife thinks I'm crazy", "I should probably just enjoy what I have", "but I really do want to play that high"

## How to use this taxonomy

When synthesizing the 5 ideas:

1. For each idea, name the PRIMARY trigger it activates (T1-T8 or a new one you discovered).
2. No two ideas in the final 5 can target the same primary trigger. If two collapse onto T4, swap one out for a different trigger.
3. If the synthesizer can't name a trigger for an idea, that idea is too vague. Reject it.
4. New triggers found in fresh research should be added to this file (with corpus quotes).

## Triggers that are NOT in this taxonomy on purpose

- "Curiosity gap" alone (too generic)
- "Social proof" alone (necessary but not sufficient)
- "Scarcity / urgency" (not Harrison's brand and not in the corpus)
- "Discount / pricing" (Harrison sells high-ticket coaching, this isn't where conversions come from)
