# Harrison Email Method  -  Talk Straight To The Reader, With Balls

> Client verdict on the old batch: *\"I do not like how you reference other people and make the entire story about them. Talk to the person READING it. It is soft cock energy. Level up.\"* This is the standing fix. Every Harrison email is now judged against it.

---

## 1. THE ONE RULE (POV)

**The READER is the protagonist of every email. Always.**

- The reader (\"you/your\") is the grammatical subject of **50-65%** of sentences.
- Harrison's first-person (\"I\") is allowed **25-40%**, but ONLY as confession in service of the reader (\"That was me at 19,\" \"I taught it wrong for years\"). Never self-aggrandizement.
- A named student/result appears in **~5-15% MAX**  -  as ONE brief proof of **2-4 lines**, then the camera whips straight back to \"you\" within one or two sentences.

**The student is NEVER the spine. The student is a mirror held up to the reader.**

The test: after any student sentence, the very next line must contain \"you/your\" and turn the student's lesson onto the reader. If it doesn't, you have written a celebrity profile. Cut it.

> BANNED openers (these built the soft batch): \"Jason told me a story...\", \"Michelle wore a night guard for five weeks...\", \"Walter is 75. He called me from his condo...\" Any email that opens by introducing a stranger is auto-rejected.

> The proof-student reattachment, done right (steal this move):
> *\"Mike played principal for 20 years and still couldn't sit above the staff without his sound cracking. I changed one thing about how his lips meet the mouthpiece. Two weeks later he was hitting high Cs cold. **Here's why that matters to you:** if you've been told 'just use more air' for 20 years and it still hasn't worked, the problem was never your air. It's the same fix.\"*

---

## 2. EDGE DEVICES (use 3-4 per email)

**a) Direct accusation  -  name what the reader is doing wrong, right now.**
> \"You've been practicing the wrong embouchure so long you've gotten really good at sounding bad.\"
> \"Every time you 'just pushed more air' to hit a high note, you trained your face to fight itself.\"

**b) Uncomfortable yes/no question that names a fear  -  fired in the first 1-3 lines, then answered FOR them.**
> \"Do you secretly wonder if your range is just gone for good at your age? It isn't. But the thing you're doing to get it back is the exact thing killing it.\"

**c) Mind-reading  -  put the reader's private, shameful thought in quotes.**
> \"You hit the wall at a G and think, 'Maybe I'm just past it. Maybe the high stuff is for younger guys now.'\"

**d) The unwelcome truth, delivered with zero cushion.**
> The CONTENT of this move stays: \"'tighten your corners and use more air' is why you've been stuck for 20 years. The method was the problem. Not you. And not your age.\"
> But the WIND-UP \"Here's the truth nobody told you:\" is BANNED as a crutch (Timo 2026-06-09, see Section 9). It got reused in almost every email. Deliver the unwelcome truth through a ROTATED transition instead:
> - Bare punch, no preamble: \"'Tighten your corners and use more air' is why you've been stuck for 20 years.\"
> - Name the villain: \"Every teacher who told you to tighten your corners had it backwards.\"
> - The flip: \"Everyone says use more air. They have it exactly backwards.\"
> - Mind-read then correct: \"You think it's your age. It's the method you were handed at 15.\"
> (Note: 'not you' is allowed HERE as a hard reframe that blames the method, NOT as soft comfort that ends the email. Edge = blame the enemy, then hand the reader the fix.)

**e) A villain  -  give the reader something to hate that isn't themselves.**
> The lie ('just use more air'), the band director who taught it, the mouthpiece-of-the-month rabbit hole, the 20 years of bad advice.

**f) Raised stakes  -  make staying the same cost something, with a clock.**
> \"Five years from now you're either playing the way you did at 25, or you've quietly stopped pulling the horn out of the case. The chops don't wait for you to decide.\"

**g) A dare  -  challenge the reader to prove it on themselves.**
> \"Try this one thing for a week. Just a week. If your high register doesn't open up, fine, I'm wrong. But you already know you won't, because you can feel it's different.\"

## 3. BE SPECIFIC IN SECOND PERSON (reader-in-a-scene)

Spend your vivid, sensory writing on the READER, not on a student. Drop them into a present-tense scene as the lead character.

> \"It's Sunday. You pull the horn out for the first time in two weeks. The first few notes feel okay. Then you reach for the G above the staff and it splatters. You set the horn down. You tell yourself you'll 'warm up more next time.' You both know you won't.\"

> \"You're 45 minutes into a gig. The lead chart hits the bridge, the range climbs, and you feel your top lip going numb. You back off. You fake it down an octave and hope the trumpet next to you covers it.\"

Build the scene from real trumpet-player specifics (the splattered G, the numb top lip, the case that stays closed, the night guard, the 6-pound horn that doesn't help). The detail makes it undeniable  -  but it has to be happening to **YOU**, the reader, not to Walter.

---

## 4. SUBJECT LINES (hard, never soft, never about a named hero)

Three modes:
- **Accusation / belief-attack:** \"You've been blowing harder for 20 years. That's the problem.\" / \"Your range didn't leave. You buried it.\"
- **Fear question:** \"Is your high register gone for good? (Read this first.)\" / \"Too old to get the top back?\"
- **Win-then-loss gut punch:** \"Principal chair. Cracking on high C.\" / \"40 years on the horn. Still scared of the bridge.\"

> BANNED: subjects naming a student as the hero (\"Jason's six-pound horn,\" \"How Michelle fixed her range\"). They flatter the wrong person and invite spectating.

---

## 5. CTA (imperative, single-destination, the READER'S move)

- Soft ladder (value emails): \"Watch the free master class where I break this down  -  [come see it here].\" Still imperative, still the reader acting.
- Hard close (sell): \"Stop guessing and go fix the actual problem. [Click here.]\" / \"Your move.\"
- One destination per email. Repeat the same link in the body close AND the P.S.
- Verb is \"come watch / come learn / apply,\" never \"buy.\" Frame the click as the reader stepping up, not purchasing.

> BANNED soft CTA: \"if you want me to look at yours.\" Permission-seeking. Rewrite to put the reader in the actor's seat: \"Want me to find what's killing your range? [Send me yours.]\"

---

## 6. THE P.S. RULE (never cloned, always a second harder sell)

**Every P.S. is written fresh for THAT email.** Across a batch, no two P.S. lines may share a template. The P.S. reframes delay as cost or dares the reader.

> \"P.S. Most guys reading this will nod, close it, and go back to blowing harder tomorrow. The ones who don't are the ones who get the top of the horn back. Which one are you?\"
> \"P.S. You've already spent 20 years doing it the hard way. What's one more week trying it right? [Here.]\"
> \"P.S. The horn doesn't get easier with age. The method does. [This is the method.]\"

> BANNED: identical P.S. across multiple drafts (the exact tell that sank the last batch). The batch-diversity gate now checks P.S. lines for shared structure and rejects clones.

---

## 7. DO / DON'T CHECKLIST

**DO**
- Make the reader the grammatical subject of the majority of sentences.
- Open with accusation, a fear question, or the reader mid-scene  -  in the first 3 lines.
- Read the reader's mind; put their private thought in quotes.
- Deliver the unwelcome truth with no cushion; label it.
- Name a villain (the failed method / the lie / the 20 years of bad advice).
- Use a student as ONE 2-4 line mirror, then snap back to \"you\" immediately.
- Raise the cost of staying the same, with a clock.
- Write every P.S. fresh as a second, harder sell.
- Keep the CTA imperative and the reader's move.

**DON'T**
- Open by introducing a named stranger (Jason/Michelle/Walter/John/Mike).
- Spend the vivid sensory scene on the student instead of the reader.
- Let a student paragraph end without turning it onto the reader.
- Cushion the reframe into comfort (\"it was never you,\" full stop).
- Clone the P.S. across the batch.
- Use a soft, permission-seeking CTA (\"if you want me to look at yours\").
- Title the email after a hero student.

---

## 8. THE GUT CHECK (run before any Harrison email ships)

1. Count it: is the reader the subject of MOST sentences? If a named other person is the spine, **REWRITE.**
2. Does the student paragraph (if any) snap back to \"you\" within one line? If not, **CUT or REATTACH.**
3. Is there a real punch in the first 3 lines aimed at the reader? If it's a polite intro, **REWRITE the open.**
4. Is the reframe edged (blame the method, dare the reader) or soft (\"it was never you,\" full stop)? Soft = **REWRITE.**
5. Is the P.S. unique to this email and harder than the body? Cloned/soft = **REWRITE.**
6. Read it as Harrison would say it out loud to a stuck 55-year-old comeback player across a table. If it sounds like a guest speaker telling a nice story about someone else, it is soft cock energy. **Level up.**"
## 9. VARY THE REFRAME TRANSITION (locked 2026-06-09, Timo)

The turn from problem to insight must NOT keep using the same wind-up. Timo: "you keep saying 'here's the truth nobody told you' in almost every email, it's ridiculous, mix it up."

BANNED as a repeated crutch (the angle gate already caps the "nobody-ever-told-you" trope at once per batch, honor it): "Here's the truth nobody told you", "Here's what nobody told you", "The truth is", "Here's the thing", "What they never tell you".

Across any batch, no two emails may use the same transition into the reframe. Rotate the MOVE itself:
- (a) Bare punch, no preamble. Just state the reframe ("The ritual built to prepare you is the thing wrecking you.").
- (b) Name the villain ("Every teacher who told you to warm up longer had it backwards.").
- (c) A question that hands them the answer ("Want to know why it quits at the end? You are out of the right kind of air.").
- (d) A flip ("Everyone says blow more air. They have it exactly backwards.").
- (e) Mind-read the reader's wrong conclusion, then correct it ("You think it's your age. It is your tongue.").
Pick a DIFFERENT one each time. If you typed "the truth nobody told you," delete it and rewrite the turn.
