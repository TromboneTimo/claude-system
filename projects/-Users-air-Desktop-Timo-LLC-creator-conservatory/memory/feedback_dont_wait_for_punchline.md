When writing copy for a Timo deck, email, or social post, do not name the *category* and stop. Write the *behavioral diagnosis* in his voice. He shouldn't have to give you the punchline if it's one inferential step from his existing framework.

**Why:** 2026-04-13 leaking-business deck — slide 8 originally said "you don't have a marketing problem, you have a dependency problem." That names the category but doesn't diagnose the behavior. Timo had to write the actual line: "youre doing things in complete reverse, and that you should be testing new ad angles organically through your email list, the comments you get on your orgnaic posts, and use that as data to validate new ad angles." Same session: "why are you not connecting ideas as well as you should and relying on me to give you all the copywriting detials." That's the failure mode.

**How to apply:**
- After writing a slide that names a problem category ("dependency", "leak", "trap"), ask: what is the SPECIFIC BEHAVIOR the audience is doing? Write that line. Example: "dependency problem" → "you're doing this in complete reverse — testing in paid before validating in organic."
- His framework has 3 patterns I can pre-apply WITHOUT asking: (1) test in organic first, paid second; (2) every paid channel needs an owned-audience fallback; (3) AI-leveraged competitors are eating un-leveraged ones.
- If a slide reads as "category named + audience nods", it's not done. The slide should make the audience flinch with self-recognition. Specific behavior > abstract pattern.
- The TIMO_PROFILE and content database EXIST so I can write his diagnoses without him hand-feeding me the line. Read them BEFORE writing, not after he corrects me.
- When in doubt about a specific behavioral claim about a real client, ASK — but for general audience diagnosis, infer from his framework.
