# The Rule

**When writing any proposal, email, pitch, blog post, or social copy that references a real person's behavior, habits, or practices — and the claim is not directly verifiable in the transcripts, memory, or prior conversation — STOP WRITING. Ask the user a targeted question. Do NOT invent a plausible-sounding claim to make the narrative smoother.**

No exceptions. Even if the pitch works better with the claim. Especially if the pitch works better with the claim — that's when the fabrication reflex fires.

# Why

2026-04-12 Otto proposal: I wrote "You already do those conversations weekly with Japan Phil conductors, Samurai Brass, Music System faculty, and international guests." Otto doesn't. Timo caught it: "otto doesnt do weekly conversations, but he could do interview series to help position hiself as a hub of brass information."

The transcript showed Otto has ACCESS to these people. My narrative pressure to make the Sarah Willis parallel land caused me to conflate "has access" with "actively does." Fabrication shipped in proposal copy. Second failure in same session (Willie Mario/Murillo was the first, pattern 4).

# The Two Claims That Must Be Separated

| Safe to write (if backed by evidence) | Requires verification |
|---|---|
| WHAT they have: network, access, skills, gear, audience, relationships | WHAT they do: behaviors, habits, routines, weekly/monthly practices |
| "Otto plays Shires" (verified in transcript) | "Otto records interviews weekly" (NOT verified) |
| "You know Japan Phil conductors" | "You interview Japan Phil conductors" |
| "You have a Substack-ready network" | "You already send a newsletter" |

# The Trigger Phrases That Require Verification

Any sentence that starts or contains:
- "You already..."
- "Since you've been..."
- "Given your weekly/monthly..."
- "As part of your regular..."
- "Like the interviews you do with..."
- "Your existing [habit/practice/content]..."

If I cannot point to specific transcript evidence or direct conversation confirming the behavior, I MUST ask. Not assume. Not infer. Not infer-confidently.

# The Ask Pattern

Before writing, when a behavioral claim feels natural but unverified:

> "Before I write this section — does Otto actually record monthly interviews with colleagues, or would this be a new behavior we're helping him start? The pitch angle changes significantly depending on the answer."

Then WAIT. Do not draft both versions speculatively. Just wait.

# Pattern Family

This is the same failure family as the 2026-04-12 Precision Brass incident (Willie Mario vs Willie Murillo, "Featured in Forbes" fabrication, prospects written as customers — all in [feedback_master_lessons.md](feedback_master_lessons.md)). Root cause is always the same: narrative convenience trumps factual accuracy.

Pattern 4 from global CLAUDE.md: "unusual-feeling claim = pause and verify." This is the same rule in different clothes. When a sentence feels "too convenient for the pitch," that's the signal.

# Applies To

- Client proposals (Timo LLC, Creator Conservatory, Precision Brass, Robinson's Remedies)
- Client emails and DMs
- Voice-bank content generation
- Blog post testimonials and case studies
- Social media copy referencing clients
- Any marketing material where a real person is named or implied
- Any "persona" content where the client's life is described

# Enforcement Test Before Any Proposal/Email Ships

1. Grep the draft for: "already", "existing", "since you've", "your weekly", "your monthly", "like you do", "given your"
2. For each hit, confirm the claim is backed by specific evidence in transcript/memory/conversation
3. If not backed: delete the sentence OR ask the user to confirm OR rewrite in future-tense framing ("we help you start X")
4. Only ship after all hits pass
