When citing a case study from a real company on a slide, the slide MUST include all 5 of these elements or it will read as unprofessional:

1. **Real brand logo** (drawn inline as SVG if no fetch is possible). NOT a fake browser tab labeled with the company name. The Microsoft squares + "bing" wordmark, the Obama 'O' with red stripes, the Highrise building icon — these are the visual anchors.
2. **One-line business context.** "Microsoft's search engine. ~$12B annual ad revenue." Audience can't be assumed to know niche brands. Even for famous brands, the line of business + scale grounds the example.
3. **Before/After visual mockup.** "X changed Y to Z" requires a side-by-side BEFORE | → | AFTER. Text description alone is insufficient. Use HTML/CSS browser/page mockups — they're reliable and on-brand.
4. **Big result number** in the slide's accent color. The headline takeaway: "+40.6%" or "+$80M / year". One focal stat per slide.
5. **Source citation** at the bottom in small caps. "Source: Ron Kohavi, HBR Sept 2017." Lets the audience verify and trust.

**Why:** 2026-04-13 first attempt used "comScore" with a fake browser tab + jargon ("CTA"). Timo: "this is unprofessional, i need the brand logo, and honestly i could use multiple companies back to back slide by slide showing results from small changes... preferable from more reputable companies than comscore, there should also be a brief description of the business." Three iteration loops to get the pattern right.

**How to apply:**
- For "small change → big lift" proof: stack 3 case study slides in sequence with brand logos. Pattern recognition lands harder when 3 different brands tell the same story.
- Pick household-name brands (Obama 2008, Microsoft, Apple, Google, Basecamp/37signals, Dropbox, Amazon) over niche industry brands (comScore, Veeam). The brand recognition does the credibility work.
- Eyebrow text on each slide should bridge: "What Marketers Know That Designers Don't" → "Same Story, Different Brand" → "The Pattern Is Everywhere". Signals to audience that this is a sequence, not random examples.
- Avoid jargon: "CTA" → "sign-up button"; "conversions" → "sign-ups". Audience is music coaches / business owners, not marketers.
- Brief = 1 line. Don't write paragraphs of business context. The 1-line description goes under the logo.

**Verified A/B test case studies (use these, they're real and well-cited):**
- Obama 2008: family photo + "Learn More" → +40.6% sign-ups, ~$60M extra. Source: Dan Siroker, Optimizely.
- Microsoft Bing: link color shade test → +$80M/year. Source: Ron Kohavi, HBR Sept 2017.
- Highrise/37signals: long-form landing page with testimonials → +37.5% sign-ups. Source: Signal v Noise blog.
- Dropbox: explainer video on homepage → significant lift (use cautiously, exact figures vary by source). Source: Drew Houston, YC interviews.

For all stats, ALWAYS cite the source on the slide itself so the user can verify. Numbers without sources read as fabrication.
