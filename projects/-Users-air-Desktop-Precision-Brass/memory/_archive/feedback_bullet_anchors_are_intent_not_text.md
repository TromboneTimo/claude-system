For Harrison long-form scripts (pb-script-write), bullet anchors are DIRECTOR'S INTENT, not draft copy. Each bullet should be one short line describing the *purpose* of that beat. What Harrison is trying to land, why it's there, what it connects to. Never write the actual sentences Harrison would say. He fills in his own words on camera.

**Why:** Harrison's voice gets killed when bullets become paragraphs of pre-written prose. Long bullets read like a Teleprompter draft and pull him out of his natural register. Timo flagged this twice in the 2026-05-06 famous-method-killing-embouchure script iteration: first on Step 3 (too many bullets), again on Step 3 (still too long). The third pass nailed it: 1-line intent bullets, no quoted prose. Locked one-liners stay verbatim, but everything else is short anchors.

**How to apply:**
- Default bullet length: ~1 line. If it wraps to 2-3 lines on screen, it's already too much.
- Each bullet says WHAT this beat does and WHY (the connection to the previous beat or the payoff for the next one).
- Locked one-liners (verbatim quotes Harrison must say word-for-word) stay long if needed. Those are the exception.
- Metaphors and reframes get a short label, not the full speech. ("Drop the marathon-with-a-weight metaphor", not the whole metaphor written out.)
- When in doubt, cut. Harrison improvises better than any draft can write for him.

This rule is layered on top of the existing protocol: locked lines verbatim, riff topics as bullets, but RIFF BULLETS = short intent labels, not pre-written paragraphs.
